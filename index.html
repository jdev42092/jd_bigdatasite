Surviving Snow:
A Study of Boston's Communication Networks

Jay Dev, Sarah Edgar, Charlotte Ong, and Maia Woluchem

Storms in Boston are bad.

And when things get really bad, there are a few places that Bostonians regularly turn to when they really need help. One of those outlets is the local BOS:311 service. Over time, that BOS:311 service has undergone a significant transformation from a largely analog phone call to more digital platforms, like the BOS:311 app or Twitter. This is a story of how that shift has changed over time, and continues to change the way we communicate during and after disasters.

Each storm is different, and so is each 311 call. So, too, are Boston's neighborhoods. Therefore, we'd expect the response from 311 to be varied by neighborhood.

Our key question: Which neighborhoods are the loudest? Which get responded to first? And what can this illuminate about how communication post-storm works around the city?

Census Tracts Sorted By Land Use & Income

Because each neighborhood in Boston is so different, each with different resources and challenges, we felt it was important to categorize them so we could see emerging patterns in 311 response. To do this, we ranked each census tract in the City of Boston by its % of residential land use and median household income. Doing this gave us four typologies of Boston tracts to ground our analysis.

Over the past four storms, most of the successfully closed cases have been analog callers.

About 70 percent of all the closed cases for the storms selected came from people using traditional phone calls, as opposed to the apps, Twitter feeds, and other technical methods the city is exploring for 311 requests. However, that's not the whole story.

Things have changed significantly over time.

As users and the city starts to roll out its BOS:311 app and other technical solutions for community engagement, the nature of this trend has changed. By Skylar, 60 percent of all successful calls for help come from digital technology.

Census Tract Analysis
To classify our census tracts into groups of comparatively high and low residential and income, we ranked each of the 179 tracts contained within the City of Boston by the percent of land dedicated to residential purposes and by their median income. The former was derived from the parcel-level Citywide Assessor's Data, published in 2016 and the latter from the 2016 5-Year American Community Survey. Based on the rankings of each variable, we gave each tract a flag determining whether it was in the top 50th percentile for each marker. Each tract was subsequently classified into a particular quadrant - one for high residential share/high income, another for high residential share/low income, and so on.


311 Analysis
Our 311 data was taken from the Analyze Boston Open Data Portal, accessed for this project on April 1st, 2018. For our analysis, we used a week's worth of information to define the universe of storm tweets: the day of the storm and the 7 days immediately following. For the analysis, we counted all types of 311 calls as "storm-related" 311 calls, regardless of the reason for the call because we were unable to unambiguously categorize each as having been related to the storm. For the average duration, this duration was calculated over only closed 311 Calls, or calls that have generally been resolved.


Twitter
We used a Twitter Scraper released on Github licensed by Free Software Inc. to scrape all tweets using certain key words and phrases. We collected three kinds of tweets: all tweets by or directed at key users such as Boston Police and Fire, tweets mentioning one of our case storms and a Boston neighborhood, tweets mentioning general weather terms like snow, flooding, or noreaster and a Boston neighborhood. Apart from the tweets that specifically mentioned a case storm, the tweets were cut to only those that occurred in the one week window of our storms. Following this step, all the tweets were brought together and cut one last time to ensure all tweets were unique.
